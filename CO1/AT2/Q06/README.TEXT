1. The overarching concept is Digital Image Formation, which is fundamentally composed of three branching processes: Image Sensing & Acquisition, Sampling (Spatial Sampling), and Quantization (Tonal Quantization).

2. Branching from Image Sensing & Acquisition, the map shows that light interacts with Optics/Lenses before reaching the Sensor (CCD/CMOS), which utilizes the Photoelectric Effect to convert photons to signal.

3. A direct causal path indicates that Image Sensing & Acquisition generates SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio), which is impacted by Sensor Noise and determines Dynamic Range.

4. Simultaneously, Optics/Lenses generate a second, independent SNR, and this combined SNR structure directly impacts Image Quality & Fidelity.

5. Following the second main branch, Sampling determines the parameters of Continuous to Discrete (Spatial) data, which in turn causes Spatial Resolution.

6. Spatial Resolution dictates performance on two downstream quality axes, leading to either successful Morarttcal definition or, conversely, Detail Loss.

7. The Sampling branch also utilizes specific mathematical principles, citing the Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem to define Critical Sampling, which is essential for successful Image Reconstruction.

8. Following the third main branch, Quantization generates Continuous to Discrete (Intensity) data and defines Bit Depth, which causes and dictates Radiometric Resolution.

9. This Quantization path highlights a negative quality trade-off: insufficient bit depth (or Uniform Quantization) requires a specific check, as it causes False Contouring and subsequent Posterization.

10. All three physical-to-discrete streams (Sensor SNR, Spatial Sampling, and Tonal Quantization) converge at the bottom, directly determining the total level of Image Quality & Fidelity.
